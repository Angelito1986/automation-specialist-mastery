1. Tell me about yourself.

I didn’t start in automation because it sounded impressive. I started because I was frustrated with repetitive work. Early in my career, I was manually updating CRMs, copying reports between systems, and fixing the same mistakes over and over. I realized the real problem wasn’t people — it was process. So I began building small automations to remove friction. Over time, that grew into designing full workflow systems across sales, operations, and marketing. Today, I focus on building scalable automation architectures that save time, reduce errors, and improve decision-making.

2. Tell me about a complex automation project you built.

One of my biggest projects involved redesigning a sales pipeline for a company losing leads due to slow response times. Leads were coming from multiple sources and manually assigned. I built an automated intake system that captured leads, enriched them, scored them, routed them dynamically, and triggered personalized follow-ups. We reduced response time from hours to under five minutes, increased conversion by 28%, and eliminated manual lead assignment entirely.

3. How do you decide what to automate?

I look at frequency, business impact, and predictability. If something happens daily, causes friction, and follows rules, it’s a strong automation candidate. I also evaluate maintenance cost. Some processes are better improved than automated. Automation should reduce complexity — not create it.

4. Describe a time automation failed in production.

I once deployed a workflow that assumed consistent API formatting. A vendor changed their API structure without notice, and the automation silently failed. That experience taught me to build validation layers, error logging, fallback paths, and monitoring alerts before going live.

5. How do you handle flaky automations?

I isolate variables. Most flaky issues come from timing, external dependencies, or inconsistent data. I add retries, logging, validation checks, and state tracking. Stability is engineered — not assumed.
